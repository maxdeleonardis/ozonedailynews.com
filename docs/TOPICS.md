# OzoneNews | Topic Intelligence Guide
**Last updated:** May 27, 2026
**Scope:** ozonedailynews.com only
**Purpose:** The topics we are obligated to cover well. Not a list of keywords. A list of things that are actually happening to the planet and to the science that explains it.

---

## Why This Document Exists

OzoneNews covers science. That means our readers come to us because they trust us to understand what is being measured, who is measuring it, and what the data actually says — not to summarize press releases or restate what a Wikipedia article already says.

Every topic below has one or more active data streams: a satellite, a research institution, an annual report, a government agency. Our job is to watch those streams and translate the numbers into clear, sourced, timestamped news that a reader can act on or learn from.

## Tier 1 | Must Cover (Active Data, High Reader Value)

These topics have live measurement programs producing new data every year — often every month. Missing them is a credibility gap.

---

### 1. Ozone Layer Recovery

The Montreal Protocol is the most successful environmental agreement in history. The ozone layer is measurably recovering — and that story is ongoing, not finished.

**Why it matters:** This is the reason the site is named OzoneNews. If we are not the best English-language source for ozone science, we have no editorial identity.

**What to watch:**
- Annual Antarctic ozone hole measurement (September/October each year) — reported by NASA and NOAA
- WMO/UNEP Scientific Assessment of the Ozone Layer (published every 4 years — next due 2026)
- Stratospheric chlorine decline data
- Recovery projections for the Arctic ozone layer (more variable than Antarctic)
- Illegal CFC-11 emissions monitoring — a real ongoing story as enforcement continues

**Primary sources:** NASA Ozone Watch, NOAA Chemical Sciences Laboratory, WMO, UNEP, British Antarctic Survey

**Article cadence:** 1-2 per month. Annual ozone hole report = major feature every October.

### 2. Space Exploration

The most active period in space history since Apollo. Multiple agencies and multiple commercial operators are running simultaneous programs with real milestones happening every quarter.

**Why it matters:** Space science generates enormous reader interest AND produces original data (new images, new discoveries, new measurements) that no one else has yet reported. First-mover advantage is real here.

**What to watch:**
- **NASA Artemis program** — Artemis III is a crewed lunar landing attempt. Every schedule update, hardware milestone, and astronaut announcement is news.
- **James Webb Space Telescope** — publishes new findings continuously. Every major paper = an article. Focus on atmospheric characterization of exoplanets (directly relevant to atmospheric science readers).
- **ESA JUICE** — Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer is en route to Jupiter. Flyby milestones are scheduled events with known dates.
- **SpaceX Starship** — orbital test flights, booster reuse records, Starbase progress. The most-watched vehicle in history.
- **Mars Sample Return** — the mission is in political and budget turmoil. Report the funding debates, not just the science.
- **Commercial launch economy** — Rocket Lab, Blue Origin, ULA, ISRO. Launch cadence records, first flights, failures.
- **Space debris** — the Kessler syndrome risk is real and tracked. Report the numbers.
- **Light pollution from satellite constellations** — Starlink and its effect on astronomical observation. Active debate in the science community.

**Primary sources:** NASA.gov news, ESA.int, SpaceX press, Rocket Lab investor calls, Space.com for aggregation (not for sourcing), arXiv for preprints

**Article cadence:** 2-3 per week when missions are active. 1 per week baseline.

### 3. Climate Science | The Data Layer

Not climate politics. Not climate opinion. The measured data — what instruments are actually recording right now.

**Why it matters:** Every journalist covers climate change. Almost no one covers the underlying data clearly and specifically. "Global temperatures rose" is not a story. "NOAA confirmed May 2026 was the 14th consecutive month above 1.5C above pre-industrial baseline" is a story.

**What to watch:**
- **Global surface temperature** — NOAA and NASA GISS publish monthly anomaly reports. Cover every one.
- **Arctic sea ice extent** — National Snow and Ice Data Center (NSIDC) publishes daily data. Annual minimum (September) and maximum (March) are major reports.
- **CO2 at Mauna Loa** — Scripps Institution of Oceanography and NOAA publish the Keeling Curve data continuously. Annual May peak reading is a significant annual milestone.
- **Ocean heat content** — NOAA publishes quarterly. The oceans are absorbing 90% of excess heat — this number matters enormously.
- **Sea level rise** — NASA satellites publish annual updates. Tide gauge data from NOAA.
- **Glacier mass balance** — World Glacier Monitoring Service publishes annual data. The 2025 report was historic.
- **Attribution science** — when a major heatwave, flood, or wildfire event occurs, climate attribution studies are published within weeks. Cover them.

**Primary sources:** NOAA, NASA GISS, NSIDC, Scripps/Keeling Curve, WMO, Copernicus Climate Change Service (EU), Berkeley Earth

**Article cadence:** Monthly data drops = immediate coverage. 1 feature per month on a specific data trend.

### 4. Atmospheric Science (Beyond Ozone)

The atmosphere is a system. Ozone is one layer of it. Our readers who care about ozone also care about air quality, wildfire smoke, stratospheric aerosols, and what goes up and what comes down.

**What to watch:**
- **Global air quality data** — IQAir publishes the World Air Quality Report annually. WHO air quality guidelines and how many cities meet them.
- **Wildfire smoke and its atmospheric impact** — smoke from large wildfires now routinely crosses continents. 2023 Canadian fires circled the globe. Track the seasons.
- **Aerosol optical depth** — NASA's MODIS and VIIRS instruments measure atmospheric particulates globally in near-real-time. A 2026 volcanic eruption anywhere in the world becomes an immediate atmospheric science story.
- **Methane in the atmosphere** — NOAA tracks atmospheric methane concentration. It is rising faster than CO2 on a warming-per-molecule basis.
- **Stratospheric aerosol injection** — geoengineering proposals to reflect sunlight are no longer theoretical. Research programs are actively being funded and debated. This is a major story with direct implications for ozone chemistry.
- **Aviation and contrails** — contrails may account for more warming than aviation CO2. This is an emerging and underreported science story.

**Primary sources:** NASA Earthdata, NOAA ESRL, IQAir, WHO, Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS)

### 5. Renewable Energy | The Numbers

Not policy debates. Not opinion on subsidies. The actual installed capacity numbers, cost curves, and grid performance data.

**What to watch:**
- **Solar and wind capacity records** — IEA publishes quarterly. Global installed solar surpassed coal capacity in 2024. The next milestones are known.
- **Grid-scale battery storage** — BloombergNEF tracks this. Price per kWh is falling. When it crosses key thresholds, it is news.
- **Nuclear energy — SMR development** — Small Modular Reactors from NuScale, TerraPower, Rolls-Royce, and others are in permitting or early construction. This is a 2026-2030 story with major milestones incoming.
- **Green hydrogen economics** — when the cost of green hydrogen crosses below grey hydrogen, it is a major energy transition milestone. We are not there yet. Report when we get closer.
- **EV adoption rates** — IEA Global EV Outlook is published annually. Monthly sales data by country.
- **Energy poverty** — the number of people globally without electricity access. Often overlooked in renewable energy coverage.

**Primary sources:** IEA, BloombergNEF, IRENA, Our World in Data, U.S. EIA

### 6. Biodiversity and Ecosystems

**What to watch:**
- **IUCN Red List updates** — published twice yearly. Every major update is a news story.
- **Great Barrier Reef bleaching surveys** — Australian Institute of Marine Science publishes annual aerial survey results.
- **Amazon deforestation rates** — Brazil's INPE publishes monthly data via PRODES. Legal deforestation under current policy is a live story.
- **Ocean deoxygenation** — low-oxygen zones ("dead zones") in the ocean are expanding. Tracked by the International Ocean Carbon Coordination Project.
- **Kelp forest mapping** — new global maps of kelp distribution are being published. Kelp is a major carbon sink.
- **Rewilding projects** — European and North American rewilding programs produce research and conservation milestones worth covering.
- **COP biodiversity summit** — COP16 targets are now being tracked for progress. Report the data, not just the pledges.

**Primary sources:** IUCN, GBIF, AIMS (Australian Institute of Marine Science), INPE, WWF Living Planet Report

---

### 7. Ocean Science

**What to watch:**
- **Ocean acidification** — pH measurements from NOAA's Global Ocean Acidification Observing Network. The chemistry is unambiguous and underreported.
- **AMOC (Atlantic Ocean circulation) stability** — the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation is weakening. Every new study on AMOC tipping points is major news.
- **Deep sea exploration** — NOAA Ocean Exploration and private expeditions are mapping the seafloor for the first time. New species, new geological features.
- **Microplastics** — now found in human blood, placentas, lung tissue, Arctic snow, Mariana Trench sediments. The measurement story is expanding rapidly.
- **Seabed 2030** — the international project to map 100% of the ocean floor by 2030. Progress reports.

---

### 8. Planetary Boundaries

The Stockholm Resilience Centre's Planetary Boundaries framework defines nine Earth system processes with quantified safe operating limits. As of 2023, six of nine boundaries had been crossed.

**What to watch:**
- Any new assessment of planetary boundary status
- The link between individual boundaries (e.g., how deforestation affects freshwater cycles, which affects climate)
- Policy responses to boundary crossings

This is a high-value topic because it gives readers a unified scientific framework for understanding all other topics on this list.

**Primary sources:** Stockholm Resilience Centre, Nature and Science journals for boundary update studies

---

### 9. Emerging Earth Science Technology

**What to watch:**
- **New satellite missions** — PACE (NASA, launched 2024, studying ocean color and atmosphere), TEMPO (monitoring air quality over North America hourly), ESA Sentinel series expansions
- **AI in climate modeling** — Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and ECMWF are using machine learning to improve weather and climate forecasts. Report the benchmark improvements.
- **CarbonMapper and methane detection** — satellite-based methane point source detection is now operational. Specific oil and gas facilities are being named. This is a major accountability story.
- **Environmental DNA (eDNA)** — water and air samples can now detect species presence from genetic traces. Revolutionizing biodiversity monitoring.

## Tier 3 | Watch and React (Breaking Events)

These are not scheduled topics but categories where breaking events require immediate coverage:

| Event Type | What triggers a story |
|---|---|
| Major volcanic eruption | Any eruption reaching the stratosphere (affects ozone and climate) |
| Extreme heat record | Any national, regional, or global temperature record |
| Wildfire season milestones | Area burned exceeding historical records |
| Mass bleaching events | Coral bleaching confirmed at scale by a research institution |
| Space mission anomaly | Any mission anomaly or loss |
| New IPCC report | Any IPCC working group report or synthesis |
| Major climate study in Nature/Science | High-impact papers in top-tier journals |
