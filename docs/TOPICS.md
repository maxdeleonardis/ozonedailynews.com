## Tech | AI Research Beat (High-Opportunity, Low Competition)

The TurboQuant article format is the template. Every article in this lane follows the same pattern: one specific paper or release, specific numbers, named researchers and institutions, a data table or benchmark comparison, and a "why this matters for production" section. No opinion. No hype. Just the data.

These articles win because:
- AI research drops daily and almost no outlet covers the actual numbers
- Developers and ML engineers search for benchmark comparisons constantly
- AI labs publish on arXiv before any journalist covers it, so first-mover advantage is real
- Google Discover heavily surfaces AI research content to technical audiences

### Article Format (always use this for Tech/AI research)

```
Title: [System/Paper Name] | [Specific Metric Result]
e.g. "TurboQuant KV Cache | 6x Memory, 8x Speed at 3-bit Precision"

Must include:
- One data table with benchmark comparisons
- The exact paper name and authors
- What problem it solves and what was impossible before
- Specific numbers: memory, speed, accuracy delta, parameter count
- What it means for production deployments
```

## Why This Document Exists

OzoneNews covers science. That means our readers come to us because they trust us to understand what is being measured, who is measuring it, and what the data actually says — not to summarize press releases or restate what a Wikipedia article already says.

Every topic below has one or more active data streams: a satellite, a research institution, an annual report, a government agency. Our job is to watch those streams and translate the numbers into clear, sourced, timestamped news that a reader can act on or learn from.

### 3. Climate Science | The Data Layer

Not climate politics. Not climate opinion. The measured data — what instruments are actually recording right now.

**Why it matters:** Every journalist covers climate change. Almost no one covers the underlying data clearly and specifically. "Global temperatures rose" is not a story. "NOAA confirmed May 2026 was the 14th consecutive month above 1.5C above pre-industrial baseline" is a story.

**What to watch:**
- **Global surface temperature** — NOAA and NASA GISS publish monthly anomaly reports. Cover every one.
- **Arctic sea ice extent** — National Snow and Ice Data Center (NSIDC) publishes daily data. Annual minimum (September) and maximum (March) are major reports.
- **CO2 at Mauna Loa** — Scripps Institution of Oceanography and NOAA publish the Keeling Curve data continuously. Annual May peak reading is a significant annual milestone.
- **Ocean heat content** — NOAA publishes quarterly. The oceans are absorbing 90% of excess heat — this number matters enormously.
- **Sea level rise** — NASA satellites publish annual updates. Tide gauge data from NOAA.
- **Glacier mass balance** — World Glacier Monitoring Service publishes annual data. The 2025 report was historic.
- **Attribution science** — when a major heatwave, flood, or wildfire event occurs, climate attribution studies are published within weeks. Cover them.

**Primary sources:** NOAA, NASA GISS, NSIDC, Scripps/Keeling Curve, WMO, Copernicus Climate Change Service (EU), Berkeley Earth

**Article cadence:** Monthly data drops = immediate coverage. 1 feature per month on a specific data trend.

### 4. Atmospheric Science (Beyond Ozone)

The atmosphere is a system. Ozone is one layer of it. Our readers who care about ozone also care about air quality, wildfire smoke, stratospheric aerosols, and what goes up and what comes down.

**What to watch:**
- **Global air quality data** — IQAir publishes the World Air Quality Report annually. WHO air quality guidelines and how many cities meet them.
- **Wildfire smoke and its atmospheric impact** — smoke from large wildfires now routinely crosses continents. 2023 Canadian fires circled the globe. Track the seasons.
- **Aerosol optical depth** — NASA's MODIS and VIIRS instruments measure atmospheric particulates globally in near-real-time. A 2026 volcanic eruption anywhere in the world becomes an immediate atmospheric science story.
- **Methane in the atmosphere** — NOAA tracks atmospheric methane concentration. It is rising faster than CO2 on a warming-per-molecule basis.
- **Stratospheric aerosol injection** — geoengineering proposals to reflect sunlight are no longer theoretical. Research programs are actively being funded and debated. This is a major story with direct implications for ozone chemistry.
- **Aviation and contrails** — contrails may account for more warming than aviation CO2. This is an emerging and underreported science story.

**Primary sources:** NASA Earthdata, NOAA ESRL, IQAir, WHO, Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS)

### Newsletter Products to Launch

#### 1. The Ozone Dispatch (Weekly)
**Format:** 5 headlines + 1 deep-read from the week's publishing  
**Cadence:** Every Friday  
**Subject line formula:** `[The Ozone Dispatch] May 30: Keeling Curve hits 430ppm, JWST finds water on K2-18b`  
**Length:** 300-500 words. Link-heavy. Fast to read.  
**Signup placement:** Homepage hero, article footer, /newsletter page

#### 2. The Data Brief (Monthly)
**Format:** One topic, 3-5 key data points, chart or table, source links  
**Cadence:** First Tuesday of each month  
**Content:** Pick one Tier 1 topic each month and go deeper than any article. Example: "May 2026 | Arctic Sea Ice: What the Numbers Actually Show"  
**Signup placement:** Offered as upgrade to Ozone Dispatch subscribers  
**Monetization potential:** Highest. Science-literate readers with disposable income. Good target for paid tier.

### What to Avoid

- **Mailchimp** — expensive at scale, not built for editorial, clunky automation
